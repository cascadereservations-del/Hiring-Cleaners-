The One Exception That Proves the Rule
Jary and LJ lasted four months — four times longer than any other hire. The key difference: they had no competing midday obligations and could respond to bookings flexibly as a unit. Available schedule, not any personal characteristic, is the single most predictive factor in cleaner retention for this role.
Root Cause Analysis

The money isn't broken.
The profile is.

Before blaming compensation, the numbers need to be tested. At ₱500 per clean with an estimated 2-hour window (checkout 12pm, check-in 2pm), the effective hourly rate is competitive — the problem lies elsewhere.

Effective Hourly Rate vs. GenSan Market
Based on 2-hour clean window per booking · GenSan/Region XII rates
₱250/hr is 4.5× the minimum wage. The rate is not the problem.
At ₱500 per clean with a 2-hour window, the cleaner earns ₱250/hr. The Region XII minimum wage is ₱55/hr. Even at the old ₱250/clean rate, the hourly earnings were ₱125/hr — still more than double minimum wage. Raising the per-clean rate alone will not fix this. It will just make the next wrong hire slightly more expensive.

The actual problem is that the role has specific scheduling constraints that do not match the life circumstances of everyone we have hired so far. The job requires midday availability on short notice — which is a real constraint that not everyone can meet, regardless of willingness.

⚠ When the schedule doesn't fit, exits follow

  • The 12pm–2pm window clashes with full daytime caregiving responsibilities
  • Short-notice calls are difficult when someone else is depending on you at that hour
  • Unexpected personal obligations cancel cleans without warning
  • When income is supplemental and competing commitments are high, work drops first
  • The role feels like a source of stress rather than a reliable earn

✓ Why Jary & LJ lasted longest (flexible schedule)

  • No competing commitments during the midday window
  • Could respond to booking notifications without conflict
  • Worked as a pair — coverage was built in from the start
  • Income contribution was meaningful without being survival-critical
  • No pattern of unexpected unavailability over four months

The Solution

Four layers. One fixed hire.
No budget increase required.

The fix is primarily a hiring decision, not a financial one. Layer 1 alone — changing who we target — is the highest-leverage action. Layers 2 through 4 are structural improvements that make any hire more likely to stay long-term.

1
Highest Impact · Zero Cost
Change who we hire

Shift sourcing away from general public postings and toward candidates within or immediately adjacent to Bria Homes. The goal is to find someone whose natural daily schedule already fits the role — not someone who has to restructure their day around it.

A neighbor inside the compound also removes transport cost entirely (saving ₱75–150 per clean) and makes reliable arrival within the 2-hour checkout-to-check-in window realistic.

The Ideal Candidate Profile
  • Lives inside or directly adjacent to Bria Homes — walking distance to Block 47
  • Age 18–28, single or with an employed partner — this income supplements the household, not replaces it
  • Has genuine flexibility during the 12pm–2pm window on weekdays — no conflicting obligations at that hour that cannot be moved
  • Currently doing light or part-time work — open to a flexible schedule with midday availability
  • Has a smartphone and is active on Messenger — for same-day booking coordination
  • Can be physically at the unit within 30 minutes of notification

3
Mutual Commitment · Zero Cost
Use a simple 90-day agreement to anchor the relationship

Every cleaner has been treated as a casual hire — paid per clean, no defined commitment from either side. That framing makes leaving easy. A simple printed or Messenger-confirmed 90-day agreement changes the psychology for both parties: the cleaner feels the role is a real position worth investing in, and the property has a mutual understanding of commitment before pressures arise.

This does not need to be a legal contract. It is a plain-language document — one page — that covers: the monthly retainer, the per-clean rate, the expected schedule, the checklist standard, and a mutual 2-week notice expectation. In Philippine culture, a written agreement carries weight precisely because it signals the employer takes the arrangement seriously. Casual pay = casual commitment.

  • Print one page, sign together on Day 1 — keep it warm and personal, not corporate ("Para malinaw tayong dalawa ang arrangement")
  • State the 90-day trial period explicitly — frame it as a two-way evaluation: "We want this to work for you too. After 90 days we'll review and adjust if needed."
  • Include the retainer amount as a specific line — seeing ₱1,500 written down as a guaranteed amount is more credible than a verbal promise
  • Add a 2-week notice clause — not punitive, but it gives Suzanne time to find coverage rather than facing a same-week gap
  • Consider a small 90-day retention bonus — ₱500 cash at the 90-day mark costs almost nothing but creates a tangible finish line to work toward
4
Operational Safety Net · Minimal Cost
Build a backup cleaner relationship before it's needed

Every cleaner resignation has caught us without coverage. This is avoidable. When the primary cleaner is hired, identify a backup person at the same time — ideally another Bria Homes neighbor — who completes one supervised clean, learns the checklist, and agrees to be available for emergency covers at ₱600–700 per clean.

This is not a second full hire. It is a one-time onboarding (one clean, one hour of walkthrough) that provides insurance against operational gaps. The backup is called only when the primary is unavailable — estimated cost is near zero per month if things are running well.

  • Find the backup at the same time as the primary — ask the primary candidate if they have a neighbor or friend who might qualify
  • One walkthrough clean together — show them the checklist app, the key code, the supply locations
  • Agree on an emergency rate — ₱600–700 per clean for backup calls (slightly above standard to motivate availability)
  • Keep the relationship warm — a monthly check-in message maintains the relationship without formal employment

Hiring Checklist

Interview questions that
screen for the right fit.

Use these during initial screening — over Messenger or in person. The goal is to surface scheduling conflicts and commitment level before hiring, not after.

  1. 1
    "Malapit ka lang ba sa Block 47? Kaya mo bang makarating within 20–30 minutes pag may biglang tawag?" ✓ Green flag: Lives in Bria Homes or within 5 minutes walk ✕ Red flag: Needs a tricycle or more than 15 minutes to arrive
  2. 2
    "Available ka ba sa weekday mornings, usually around 12nn–2pm? Minsan may short notice, ilan oras lang babahan." ✓ Green flag: Says yes readily, no visible hesitation about that time slot ✕ Red flag: "Depende sa sitwasyon" or pauses and names a recurring conflict at that hour
  3. 3
    "Kung may biglang tawag kami sa 12nn, wala ka bang commitment na hindi mo maiwanan? Importante kasi talaga ang availability sa oras na iyon." ✓ Green flag: Answers clearly and without hesitation — confirms that time is genuinely free ✕ Red flag: Describes a regular responsibility at that hour with no one to cover it
  4. 4
    "Nagawa mo na bang mag-Airbnb cleaning o hotel-style cleaning dati? Comfortable ka ba mag-follow ng strict checklist at mag-send ng photos?" ✓ Green flag: Previous experience or expresses genuine interest in the standard ✕ Red flag: Dismissive of photo requirements or "kaya ko naman iyan kahit walang checklist"
  5. 5
    "Okay lang ba sa iyo ang monthly arrangement? May guaranteed ₱1,500 retainer ka every month, tapos ₱500 per cleaning on top of that." ✓ Green flag: Asks follow-up questions about how many cleans per month — shows they are calculating ✕ Red flag: Only interested in the per-clean rate and not the retainer — signals they plan to keep it casual
  6. 6
    "Kung may biglang personal matter — halimbawa may sakit sa pamilya — may kakilala ka bang makakapalit sa iyo for that day?" ✓ Green flag: Names someone immediately, ideally a neighbor who could also become the backup ✕ Red flag: "Wala talaga, ako lang makakapalit sa sarili ko" — no built-in redundancy
